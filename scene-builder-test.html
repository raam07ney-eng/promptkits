About AI Scene Builder

AI Scene Builder gives you director-level control over every shot in your AI video scene. Instead of asking Kling AI to generate a full sequence automatically — where it invents the lens choices, the cutting rhythm, and the light direction — you define every technical decision yourself and generate each shot individually with professional cinematography language.

Every shot gets its own lens, camera movement, and action description. A shared Scene Bible feeds into every prompt automatically so your scene stays consistent from the wide establishing shot to the final resolved frame. Fixed Elements lock light direction, props, and background details globally across all shots.

Built by a working VFX artist with 12 years of industry experience — the same shot-by-shot approach used in professional film production.

Why shot-by-shot control produces better AI video

AI video models are trained on enormous libraries of real cinematography. They understand lens compression, camera movement, depth of field, and lighting quality — but only when you give them the right language to work with. A prompt that says "cinematic shot of two people talking" gives the model nothing to anchor on. A prompt that specifies a 50mm lens, over-the-shoulder framing at eye level, camera locked off, single window light from the left, and a specific character expression — that gives the model a complete visual instruction it can execute with precision.

The fundamental problem with auto multi-shot generation is that the AI has to invent the editorial decisions that a human director would make. It invents the coverage, the cutting rhythm, the lens choices, and the light direction. Sometimes it gets lucky. More often, the result is generic — the kind of footage that looks like AI because no real director would cut that way.

Scene Builder is built on the opposite principle: the director makes every decision, and the AI executes it. You choose which moment gets the close-up. You decide when the camera pushes in. You lock the light source and the axis. The AI's job is to translate your visual instructions into a prompt that produces exactly the frame you designed.

Common questions

FAQ

Why does the light direction keep changing between shots?
Kling AI has no memory between clips — every shot is a fresh generation from scratch. It reinterprets your scene setup each time, including the light source position. The fix is the Fixed Elements field — write your light direction there and it gets injected into every single shot prompt automatically. Example: "Window light always from the LEFT wall — left side of every face always lit, right side always in shadow."
Why is Kling generating multiple cuts in one clip when I only want one shot?
This happens when the shot description contains more than one action or a sequence of events. Kling reads narrative structure and creates cuts automatically. Keep the Action / Emotion / Dialogue field to a single moment — one gesture, one held expression, one action. For the Wide Establishing shot especially, describe only the starting state of the scene, not what will happen during it.
What's the best way to keep characters consistent across shots?
Two things work together: (1) Reference image — generate a Midjourney reference frame for each shot and attach it in Kling. (2) Previous shot's last frame — pause your last Kling clip at the final second, screenshot it, and use that as the reference for the next shot. This carries over exact clothing, hand positions, and prop states. For large lens jumps (18mm to 85mm), generate a fresh Midjourney reference instead of using the previous frame.
Should I use 5 seconds or 10 seconds per shot in Kling?
5 seconds for static shots — close-ups, OTS coverage, insert shots, any shot where the camera is locked off. 10 seconds for shots with camera movement (push in, pull back, tracking, crane) or long dialogue sequences. Kling needs the full duration to complete a movement cleanly. Going shorter on a movement shot gives you an incomplete camera move.
Why does my prop keep changing position between shots?
Add it to Fixed Elements with its exact position. Be specific: "Coffee cup always on the right side of the table in front of Character 2 — never moves." Also use the previous shot's last frame as your reference image — the prop position from the last clip carries into the next one.
What is the 180-degree rule and why does it matter?
The 180-degree rule means the camera always stays on one side of an imaginary line drawn between two characters. If the camera crosses this line, the characters appear to have swapped positions on screen — which breaks spatial continuity for the viewer. In Scene Builder, put this in Fixed Elements: "Camera always on the RIGHT side of the table axis — never crosses to the left side." Every shot prompt will then respect this rule.
Does Scene Builder work with Runway and other AI video tools?
Yes. The prompts are written in professional cinematography language that works across Kling AI, Runway Gen-4, Google Veo/Flow, Pika, and Hailuo. The Reference Image tab outputs a Midjourney prompt optimised for static frames. The video prompt works with any AI video model — Kling is recommended because it responds best to detailed cinematic language.
How is Scene Builder different from the Video Prompt Generator?
The Video Prompt Generator creates a single prompt for a single clip — good for standalone shots, hero moments, or social content. Scene Builder is for multi-shot scenes — it maintains a shared Scene Bible across all shots, locks consistency elements globally, and generates both a video prompt and a Midjourney reference image for each individual shot. Use Scene Builder whenever you need more than one clip to work together as a sequence.
Step by step

How to use AI Scene Builder

Fill the Scene Bible once — generate each shot individually. The whole scene is consistent because the context never changes.

1

Fill the Scene Bible

Describe the location in detail, set the time of day, lighting, atmosphere, colour grade, and mood. This context feeds into every single shot prompt — you write it once and it applies everywhere.

💡 Be specific about location — "small coffee shop corner table, warm wooden interior, single window on the left wall" is better than "coffee shop"
2

Set Fixed Elements

Use the Fixed Elements field for anything that must stay consistent across every shot — prop positions, light source direction, background details. These are injected into every prompt automatically.

💡 Always lock your light source direction here — "window light always from the LEFT, left side of faces lit, right side in shadow"
3

Add Characters

Describe each character's appearance in detail — age, build, skin tone, hair, clothing, accessories. Optionally upload a reference photo to auto-extract the appearance description. Character 2 starts disabled — click Add Character to enable.

💡 Upload a reference photo for each character — the AI extracts their exact appearance and fills the field automatically
4

Select and configure shots

Check the shots you want. Each shot expands to show Lens, Camera Movement, and Action/Emotion/Dialogue fields. Set the lens and movement for each shot individually — then describe exactly what happens in that specific frame.

💡 The Action field drives the prompt — write only what happens in this single moment, not the full scene story
5

Generate Reference Image first

For each shot, use the Reference Image tab to generate a Midjourney prompt. Generate the static frame in Midjourney first — then use that image as your reference in Kling AI. This is the single most important step for visual consistency.

💡 Use the previous shot's last frame as the reference for the next shot — characters and props carry over naturally
6

Generate Video Prompt and produce in Kling

Switch to the Video Prompt tab and generate. Copy the prompt into Kling AI, attach your reference image, set duration (5s for most shots, 10s for movement shots and dialogue), and generate. Only regenerate the shots that need fixing — everything else stays.

💡 5 seconds for static shots, 10 seconds for camera movement shots and dialogue sequences

Tips for better output

These are the differences between a Kling output that looks like AI and one that looks like it was shot on a professional camera.

🔒

Lock the light direction in Fixed Elements

Light direction is the most common consistency failure. Kling will reinterpret it in every shot unless you lock it globally.

Window light always from the LEFT wall. Left side of every face always lit. Right side always in shadow.
🖼

Reference image = previous shot's last frame

After generating a clip in Kling, pause at the last second and screenshot it. Use that as the reference for the next shot. Props, hands, clothing all carry over.

✂️

One action per shot description

Describe only what happens in this single moment. Multiple actions in one shot description causes Kling to generate a multi-shot sequence.

✗ "She enters, sits down, opens the folder"
✓ "She seated, folder closed in front of her, watching him in silence"
📐

Keep the camera on one side of the axis

For dialogue scenes, decide which side of the table/axis the camera stays on — and never cross it. Put this in Fixed Elements so every prompt respects it.

Camera always on the RIGHT side of the table axis throughout all shots.
⏱

5 seconds for statics, 10 for movement

Static shots work best at 5s. Any shot with camera movement — push in, pull back, tracking — needs 10s for Kling to complete the movement cleanly.

🎬

Don't regenerate what works

If 5 of 7 shots are perfect, only regenerate the 2 that need fixing. Start with the hardest shots first — wide establishing and any shot with two characters moving.

👤

Translate dialogue into performance

Kling AI sees visuals, not words. Describe what the camera sees during the dialogue — not the words themselves.

✗ "She says: I knew it was you"
✓ "She leans forward and speaks, voice low, eyes fixed — a statement not a question"
🌅

Describe lighting quality not just type

Don't just say "golden hour" — describe the quality. Kling responds to specific descriptions of what the light does to the scene.

Warm amber side light raking across both faces from the left, long shadows stretching right, highlights catching the rim of the coffee cups
🏠

Location detail prevents drift

The more specific your location description, the less Kling invents. Name specific details — wall colour, floor material, what's visible in the background.

Worn wooden table, exposed brick far wall, bookshelf behind the right character, soft-focus background patrons
