About Scene Builder

Scene Builder gives you director-level control over every shot in your AI video scene. Instead of asking an AI model to generate a full sequence automatically — where it invents the lens choices, the cutting rhythm, and the light direction — Scene Builder lets you define every technical decision yourself and generate each shot individually with professional cinematography language.

Every shot gets its own lens, camera movement, and action description. A shared Scene Bible — location, lighting, atmosphere, colour grade, and mood — feeds into every prompt automatically so your scene stays consistent from the wide establishing shot to the final resolved frame. Fixed Elements lock props, light direction, and background details across all shots so nothing drifts between clips.

Scene Builder is built by a working VFX compositor and is based on the same shot-by-shot approach used in professional film production. The result is AI video that looks like it was directed, not generated.

How to Use Scene Builder

1
Fill the Scene Bible
Describe the location in detail, set the time of day, lighting, atmosphere, colour grade, and mood. This context feeds into every single shot automatically — write it once and it applies everywhere.
2
Add Fixed Elements
Use the Fixed Elements field to lock anything that must stay consistent across every shot — light source direction, prop positions, background details, camera axis. Example: "Window light always from the LEFT wall. Left side of every face always lit, right side in shadow."
3
Add your characters
Describe each character's appearance in detail. Upload a reference photo to auto-extract the appearance description. Start with one character — click Add Character to enable more, up to four.
4
Select shots and set per-shot details
Check the shots you want. Each shot expands to show Lens, Camera Movement, and Action/Emotion/Dialogue fields. Set the lens and movement individually for each shot, then describe exactly what happens in that single frame only.
5
Generate Reference Image first
For each shot, use the Reference Image tab to generate a Midjourney prompt. Generate the static frame in Midjourney first — then use that image as your reference in Kling AI. This is the single most important step for character and scene consistency.
6
Generate video prompts and produce in Kling AI
Switch to the Video Prompt tab and generate. Copy into Kling AI, attach your reference image, set duration (5s for static shots, 10s for movement shots), and generate. Regenerate only the shots that need fixing — everything else stays.

Tips for Better Output

✦
Lock light direction in Fixed ElementsWrite your light source direction once in Fixed Elements — it injects into every shot automatically. Without this, Kling reinterprets the light in every clip.
✦
Use previous shot's last frame as referencePause your last Kling clip at the final second, screenshot it, and use it as the reference for the next shot. Clothing, hand positions, and props carry over exactly.
✦
One action per shot descriptionDescribe only what happens in this single moment. Multiple actions cause Kling to generate a multi-shot sequence with unwanted cuts.
✦
5 seconds for statics, 10 for movementStatic shots work best at 5s in Kling AI. Any shot with camera movement needs 10s for Kling to complete the move cleanly without cutting it short.
✦
Translate dialogue into visible performanceKling AI sees visuals not words. Describe what the camera physically sees: "she leans forward and speaks, jaw set, eyes fixed" not the actual dialogue line.
✦
Camera axis in Fixed ElementsFor dialogue scenes, lock the camera axis: "Camera always on the RIGHT side of the table axis — never crosses left." This maintains the 180-degree rule across all shots.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does the light direction keep changing between shots?
Kling AI has no memory between clips — every shot is a fresh generation. Add your light direction to the Fixed Elements field: "Window light always from the LEFT wall. Left side of every face always lit, right side in shadow." This injects into every prompt automatically and locks the light across the entire scene.
Why is Kling generating multiple cuts inside one clip?
This happens when the Action/Emotion/Dialogue field contains more than one action or a sequence of events. Kling reads narrative structure and creates cuts automatically. Keep the description to a single moment — one gesture, one held expression, one starting state. Describe where the action begins, not everything that happens during the clip.
How do I keep characters consistent across all shots?
Two things work together. First, generate a Midjourney reference image for each shot and attach it in Kling. Second, use the previous shot's last frame as the reference for the next shot — pause at the final second, screenshot it, and use that. This carries over exact clothing, hand positions, and prop states between every clip.
Does Scene Builder work with Runway and other AI video tools?
Yes. The prompts are written in professional cinematography language that works across Kling AI, Runway Gen-4, Google Veo/Flow, Pika, and Hailuo. Kling AI is recommended as it responds best to detailed cinematic language. The Reference Image tab outputs a Midjourney prompt optimised for static frames usable as reference in any tool.
What is the 180-degree rule and why does it matter?
The 180-degree rule means the camera always stays on one side of an imaginary line between two characters. Crossing this line makes the characters appear to swap positions on screen, breaking spatial continuity. Lock it in Fixed Elements: "Camera always on the RIGHT side of the table axis — never crosses to the left side." Every shot prompt will then respect this rule automatically.
How is Scene Builder different from the Video Prompt Generator?
The Video Prompt Generator creates a single prompt for a single standalone clip — ideal for hero shots or social content. Scene Builder is for multi-shot scenes — it maintains a shared Scene Bible across all shots, locks consistency globally via Fixed Elements, and generates both a video prompt and a Midjourney reference image for each individual shot. Use Scene Builder whenever you need more than one clip to work together as a scene.

Why shot-by-shot control produces better AI video

AI video models understand cinematography — lens compression, camera movement, depth of field, lighting quality — but only when you give them the right language. A prompt that says "cinematic shot of two people talking" gives the model nothing to anchor on. A prompt that specifies a 50mm lens, over-the-shoulder framing at eye level, camera locked off, single window light from the left, and a specific character expression gives the model a complete visual instruction it can execute with precision.

The fundamental problem with auto multi-shot generation is that the AI has to invent the editorial decisions a human director would make. It invents the coverage, the cutting rhythm, the lens choices, and the light direction. Sometimes it gets lucky. More often the result is generic — footage that looks like AI because no real director would cut that way.

Scene Builder is built on the opposite principle: you make every decision and the AI executes it. You choose which moment gets the close-up. You decide when the camera pushes in. You lock the light source and the axis. The AI translates your visual instructions into exactly the frame you designed.
