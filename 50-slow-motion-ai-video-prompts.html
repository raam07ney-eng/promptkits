About These Slow-Motion Prompts

Slow motion is where AI video quietly outperforms real production. A genuine high-speed camera costs lakhs and demands blinding light; an AI model renders a water crown or a fabric wave at any imagined frame rate for free. The catch is knowing what to ask for — vague "slow motion" prompts produce sluggish normal footage, while the prompts on this page specify the exact physics the model should exaggerate: droplet crowns, rippling cheeks, spiralling dust, two-speed debris.

Every prompt follows the rules that keep AI slow motion coherent: one event per shot, physics described in detail (because the detail language is what forces the model into true slow-motion rendering), and subjects chosen from what models render best — liquids, particles, fabric, and single-contact impacts. Faces appear mostly as reaction shots, where slow motion flatters AI rather than exposing it.

The collection covers the six families of the speed-ramp toolkit: fight impacts, reaction beats, glass and debris, water and liquid, fabric and dust, and sports moments with hero entrances. Pair them with normal-speed shots using the three-shot ramp method explained below, and you have the full John Wick grammar.

Tips for Best Results

✦
Say what the slow motion reveals, not just "slow motion" — "the impact crown rising, a rebound column with a droplet balanced on top" forces true slo-mo rendering; a bare "slow motion" tag often just produces slow-feeling normal footage.
✦
One event per shot. "The punch connects" works; "he punches, dodges and counters in slow motion" collapses. Chain events across separate generations and cut them together.
✦
Particles are your friends: dust, spray, chalk, confetti and embers give the model something to render slowly — a slow-motion shot with nothing airborne in it rarely reads as slow.
✦
For speed ramps, generate the normal-speed and slow-motion shots with identical characters, wardrobe and lighting descriptions — any drift between them breaks the cut. Lock the details once and reuse them word-for-word.

How to Build a Speed Ramp with AI Video

The John Wick moment — normal speed, then time collapses on the impact, then snaps back — is not one AI generation. Prompting "suddenly slow motion" inside a single clip produces mush, because models can't change their own frame timing mid-generation. Real films don't do it in one take either: a speed ramp is built in the edit, and the AI version works the same way.

The three-shot method: (1) generate your action beat at normal speed — the swing, the leap, the throw. (2) Generate the peak moment as its own dedicated slow-motion shot using the prompts on this page — this is where AI genuinely excels, because the whole clip has one consistent speed. (3) In your editor, hard-cut into the slow shot at the impact frame and cut back out after 1–2 seconds. A bass hit or audio drop on the cut does half the work.

A VFX artist's note on slowing footage further: if you stretch an AI clip in your editor beyond about 50%, it stutters — the frames simply don't exist. Use optical-flow interpolation (Premiere's Optical Flow, DaVinci's Speed Warp) to synthesise the missing frames, and it works best on the exact subjects this page favours: droplets, dust, fabric — high-detail motion against clean backgrounds. But whenever possible, prompt the slow motion instead of manufacturing it in post: the model renders true slow-motion detail (droplet crowns, fabric waves) that no interpolation can invent. Frequently Asked Questions

Can AI video do a speed ramp inside one clip?
Not reliably. Models generate at one internal tempo per clip, so "suddenly slow motion" mid-prompt usually produces inconsistent mush. The professional workaround — used on this page — is the three-shot method: normal-speed shot, dedicated slow-motion shot of the peak moment, hard cut between them in your editor. That is also how real films build speed ramps.
Why does my "slow motion" prompt just look like slow, boring footage?
Because the model needs to know what detail the slow motion exists to reveal. Add the physics: droplet crowns, rippling fabric, dust ejecting, a cheek deforming on impact. Detail language is what switches the model from "slow pace" to true high-speed-camera rendering. Every prompt in this collection is built around one such detail.
Should I prompt slow motion or slow the clip down in editing?
Prompt it when the shot is about physics detail — water, glass, fabric — because the model renders detail that frame interpolation cannot invent. Slow it in post (using Optical Flow or Speed Warp, never a plain speed change) when you need precise timing control for a music cut. Many editors do both: prompt at slow motion, then fine-tune ±20% in the timeline.
How do I keep my character identical between the normal and slow-motion shots?
Use locked identity descriptors — exact clothing colours, hair, distinctive details — repeated word-for-word in both prompts, ideally with the same reference image.
