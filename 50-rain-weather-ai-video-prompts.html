About These Rain & Weather Prompts

Weather is the fastest way to give an AI shot a mood — the same street reads as romance in a sun shower, menace in fog, and melancholy in steady rain. It is also one of the most reliable things AI video renders, because weather is texture and light rather than anatomy: no faces to keep consistent, no hands to draw. A well-prompted storm frame can pass for real footage more easily than almost any other AI shot.

But weather prompts fail in one specific, predictable way: rain that touches nothing, snow that never settles, wind that moves no fabric. The fix is the interaction rule explained below — every prompt in this collection describes the weather meeting the world: crowns bursting on glass, water sheeting off shoulders, gust waves racing through wheat, frost growing crystal by crystal.

The 50 prompts span six families — rain, storms and lightning, snow and cold, fog and mist, wind-heat-sun, and interior weather moods — with a deliberate share of monsoon, chai-stall and terrace imagery for creators telling South Asian stories, alongside the universal cinematic staples.

Tips for Best Results

✦
Follow the interaction rule: never prompt weather alone — prompt weather touching things. "Heavy rain" is weak; "rain sheeting off his shoulders, drops bursting on the wet stone" renders.
✦
Wet the ground. "Rain-slicked asphalt reflecting every light" doubles your production value and instantly sells the scene — a dry-floored rain shot reads as fake no matter how good the streaks are.
✦
Match weather across every shot of a scene: same intensity, same grey light, same soaked surfaces. Treat it as a Fixed Element, written identically into every prompt.
✦
Fog and snow are the safest weathers for character shots — they soften detail and hide small inconsistencies — while backlit rain and lightning are the most dramatic but least forgiving. Choose the weather for the job.

Making AI Rain Look Real: The Interaction Rule

The most common AI rain failure is rain that falls but touches nothing — streaks pasted over a dry-looking scene. Real rain is defined by its interactions, and the model only renders those if the prompt demands them. Every rain prompt on this page follows the interaction rule: name at least two of — water sheeting off shoulders, drops bursting into micro-crowns, runnels racing on glass, puddles doubling every light source, steam rising from warm surfaces, fabric darkening as it soaks.

Wet ground is half the shot. A rain scene where the ground stays matte reads as fake instantly. Always specify the surface state: "rain-slicked asphalt reflecting the neon," "flooded street turned to a mirror," "wet stone glistening." Reflections are free production value — they double every light in your scene and hide small inconsistencies in the process.

Weather is a Fixed Element. If your scene spans multiple shots, the rain's intensity, the light's greyness and the wetness of every surface must match across all of them — one dry-pavement shot breaks the entire sequence. Lock the weather once ("steady heavy rain, overcast flat light, all surfaces soaked and reflective") and inject it into every prompt — our free AI Scene Builder does exactly this through its Fixed Elements field.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does AI rain often look fake or pasted-on?
Because the prompt asked for rain but not for its consequences. Models render "rain" as streaks unless you describe the interactions — drops bursting into crowns, water running off surfaces, clothes darkening, puddles reflecting light. Add two or three interactions and the same scene transforms. This is the interaction rule every prompt on this page is built around.
Can AI video do lightning realistically?
Yes — with two tricks. First, prompt the consequence, not just the bolt: the one-frame overexposure, every wet surface flashing white, the window-frame shadow printed on a wall. Second, keep one strike per shot; multiple strikes tend to merge into flicker. For storm sequences, cut separate strike shots together in editing.
How do I keep the weather consistent across a multi-shot scene?
Write the weather as a locked block — "steady heavy rain, overcast flat grey light, all surfaces soaked and reflective" — and paste it identically into every shot prompt. One shot with dry pavement or sunny light breaks the whole sequence. Our free AI Scene Builder automates this with its Fixed Elements field, which injects your weather description into every shot.
Which AI tool handles weather best?
Kling AI currently leads on water physics — rain interactions, puddle reflections and wet-surface rendering — which is why most prompts here are tuned for it. Runway excels at fog volumetrics and slow-motion droplets. All 50 prompts use standard cinematography language and work across Kling, Runway, Sora, Pika and Hailuo.
